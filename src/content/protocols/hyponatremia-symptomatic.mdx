## 🎯 When to treat urgently

### Severe symptomatic — CNS signs

Any of these + Na &lt; 120:
- **Seizure**
- **Coma** (GCS &lt; 8)
- **Severe confusion / AMS**
- **Respiratory distress** (neurogenic)
- **Vomiting + headache** (↑ ICP features)

→ **Immediate hypertonic saline**

### Moderate — treat based on context

- Na &lt; 125 + headache, N/V, mild confusion
- Decide treatment pace based on chronicity

### Level categories

| Na (mEq/L) | Severity |
|---|---|
| 135–145 | Normal |
| **130–134** | Mild |
| **125–129** | Moderate |
| **&lt; 125** | Severe |
| **&lt; 120** | Very severe (CNS risk) |

---

## ⏱ Acute vs Chronic — critical distinction

### Acute (&lt; 48 hr documented)

- Brain does not have time to adapt
- Severe CNS edema possible
- Can correct faster (lower ODS risk)
- Example: post-op SIADH, psychogenic polydipsia, MDMA

### Chronic (&gt; 48 hr or unknown duration)

- Brain has adapted (lost solutes)
- **Rapid correction → osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS)**
- Rule: **Na rise ≤ 8–10 mEq in 24 hr**, ≤ 18 mEq in 48 hr
- Example: CHF, cirrhosis, SIADH chronic, hypothyroid

### ODS (Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome)

- Delayed onset 2–6 days after correction
- Locked-in, dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis
- Often irreversible — **avoid at all costs**
- Risk factors: alcoholism, malnutrition, hypokalemia, liver disease

## 🧪 Workup — determine cause

### Key questions

1. **Volume status:** hypovolemic, euvolemic, hypervolemic?
2. **Serum osmolality:** true hypotonic hyponatremia?
3. **Urine osmolality + urine Na:** narrow differential

### Labs

- BMP, Mg, Ca, glucose, LFT, TSH, cortisol (AM)
- **Serum osmolality**
- **Urine Na, urine osm** (spot)
- Uric acid

### Differential by volume + urine

| Volume | Urine Na | Cause |
|---|---|---|
| Hypovolemic | &lt; 20 | GI/skin loss (diarrhea, sweat) |
| Hypovolemic | &gt; 20 | Renal loss (diuretic, CSW, adrenal insuff) |
| Euvolemic | &gt; 20 | **SIADH**, hypothyroid, glucocorticoid def |
| Hypervolemic | &lt; 20 | CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic |
| Hypervolemic | &gt; 20 | CKD, AKI |

### Pseudohyponatremia

- Hyperglycemia: Na corrected = measured + 1.6 per 100 glucose above 100
- Hyperlipidemia, paraproteinemia: check serum osm

## 💊 Acute Treatment — Symptomatic

### Severe symptomatic (seizure, coma, severe AMS)

**Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) bolus**

- **100 mL IV over 10 min**
- Can **repeat × 2** (up to 3 × 100 mL) until symptoms improve
- Total ~ 300 mL typically raises Na by ~ 4–6 mEq/L

**Target:**
- Resolve symptoms (usually Na rise 4–6 mEq is enough)
- Then switch to slower correction per chronicity

**Monitor:**
- Na q 1–2 hr initially
- Symptoms (GCS, seizure cessation)

### After initial bolus — slow correction

**Na rise limit:**
- **≤ 8–10 mEq in 24 hr** (safer: 6–8)
- **≤ 18 mEq in 48 hr**
- If acute onset clearly &lt; 24 hr: can go faster, but still ≤ 12 mEq/24 hr

**Options depending on cause:**
- **Hypovolemic:** NSS 0.9% infusion
- **Euvolemic (SIADH):** Fluid restriction 800–1000 mL/d ± furosemide + salt
- **Hypervolemic:** Fluid + salt restriction, diuretic

### ❌ Over-correction — relower if needed

If Na rise &gt; 10 mEq in 24 hr:
- **D5W 3 mL/kg/hr** to lower Na back
- **DDAVP 2–4 mcg IV** (prevent further water loss)
- Consult nephrology

## 📊 การติดตาม (Monitoring)

- **Na q 1–2 hr** during acute correction; q 4 hr once stable
- Neuro checks q 1 hr
- Urine output (watch aquaresis if DI develops)
- Seizure precautions
- K, Mg (replace concurrently — low K worsens ODS risk)

## ⚠️ ข้อควรระวัง (Critical Pitfalls)

```
🚩 Don't correct &gt; 10 mEq / 24 hr
   - ODS risk — often irreversible
   - Safer target: 6–8 mEq / 24 hr
   - Alcoholics, liver dz, K-depleted = higher risk

🚩 Use 3% NaCl for severe sx, not normal saline
   - NSS may not raise Na fast enough
   - 3% 100 mL bolus simple + effective

🚩 SIADH + NSS can WORSEN Na
   - If urine osm &gt; 2× serum osm, NSS excretes Na + retains water
   - Use hypertonic or fluid restriction

🚩 Check glucose first (pseudohyponatremia)
   - Hyperglycemia: correct Na before treating
   - Every 100 mg/dL above 100 → add 1.6 to Na

🚩 Replace K concurrently
   - Hypokalemia → ODS risk ↑
   - K repletion itself raises Na
   - Monitor closely if giving both

🚩 Over-correction: relower Na
   - Rising too fast (&gt; 10 in 24 hr) = give D5W
   - DDAVP if diuresis high
   - Don't wait — act immediately

🚩 Thiazide-induced hyponatremia
   - Stop thiazide
   - Volume repletion with NSS
   - Often quick resolution

🚩 Post-op + HTS + seizure
   - Often SIADH post-surgery
   - Low threshold for 3% NaCl
```

## 📝 คำสั่งพร้อมใช้ (Copy-Ready Order)

```
Dx: Symptomatic hyponatremia, Na = ___ mEq/L [acute / chronic]
Sx: [seizure / coma / AMS / headache-vomiting]

1. IV × 1; continuous monitor; seizure precautions
2. STAT labs: BMP, serum osm, urine Na, urine osm, glucose
   Also: Mg, Ca, TSH, cortisol, LFT
3. Assess volume status (exam + hx)
4. SEVERE SYMPTOMATIC (seizure, coma, severe AMS):
   - 3% NaCl 100 mL IV over 10 min
   - Repeat × 2 (total up to 300 mL) until symptoms improve
   - Check Na q 1 hr
   - Goal: symptom resolution, Na rise 4–6 mEq
5. After initial rise — maintain slow correction:
   - Target: Na rise &lt; 8–10 mEq / 24 hr (chronic)
   - Acute documented &lt; 24 hr onset: can be faster but cautious
6. Cause-specific:
   - Hypovolemic: NSS 0.9% infusion
   - SIADH (euvolemic): fluid restriction 800 mL/d ± furosemide + salt tabs
   - Hypervolemic (CHF, cirrhosis): fluid + salt restriction, loop diuretic
7. Replace K concurrently if &lt; 3.5 (+ Mg)
8. OVER-CORRECTION safety net:
   - If Na rise &gt; 10 mEq / 24 hr: start D5W 3 mL/kg/hr + DDAVP 2 mcg IV
   - Relower Na back
9. Stop offending meds (thiazide, SSRI, carbamazepine)
10. Seizure management if active:
    - Benzo + 3% NaCl concurrent
    - Correct cause simultaneously
11. Consult:
    - Nephrology (complex cases, over-correction, refractory)
12. Admit ICU (severe) or medical floor with q 2 hr Na
```
